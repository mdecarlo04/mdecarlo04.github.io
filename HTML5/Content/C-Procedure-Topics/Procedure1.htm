What is accessibility?

Two models of disabilities

There are two ways in which we interact with the concept of disabilities: the “medical model” and the “social model.” The medical model is the main way in which we interact with disabilities. This model views disabilities as falling under an established threshold. There are established thresholds which indicate “normal” functioning, but falling below this threshold indicates someone having a disability. While this model is useful in the medical sense of determining and diagnosing someone with a disability, it is not effective as the main way for people to interact with disabilities. For the everyday person, this model perpetuates stereotypes against people with disabilities as it creates a measure in which they are different from a “normal” person.

The social model views the environment as disabling the person. Stairs are disabling a person to get into the building, rather than the person with a wheelchair. This completely shifts the way we look at disabilities and accessibility as we will look at the world as needing to adapt to people, rather than someone needing to get by and adapt to their surroundings. It is the building and the people who designed the building’s fault for not having a ramp and elevator. We should expect a building or a website to have features that make it accessible for all users, rather than expect someone to figure it out themselves. Society is to blame for the shortcomings in regards to anyone with disabilities.

Digital accessibility

Digital accessibility is the ability of all electronic content to be used by everyone with the same ease of use, especially for people with disabilities. It is also a legal requirement in the United States under the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990. This means that any online platform must be made accessible for everyone, regardless of their abilities.

In order to ensure complete accessibility, there are resources available for web content developers and anyone else who works with electronic content. WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) is used as a reference point for standards in accessibility and has a set of recommendations in making web content usable by everyone. Their guidelines are organized into 4 main categories: perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. If any of these 4 categories cannot be said about your web content, then it is not accessible.

Accessible design versus universal design

When creating accessible web content, there are two different design types: accessible design and universal design. These two types have a very minute, but key difference. Accessible design considers creating features for people with disabilities. For example, after constructing a building, someone makes the decision to build a ramp in the back of the building. Even though we should be considering everybody’s needs, accessible design treats accessibility as if it’s an afterthought. Ramps are important, but creating them all the way in the back of the building where it is much more of a hassle to reach should not be the standard.

Universal design considers the needs of everyone and aims to make their products/creations accessible for the widest possible audience. This design approach considers accessibility from the beginning and works to ensure that their product will be usable by the most amount of people possible. Instead of building a ramp in the back of a building at the end of the construction process, people start the construction process considering accessibility. They might combine both a ramp and stairs at the front of the building. This would mean that not only people who use wheelchairs or walkers to get around can use it, but also parents pushing strollers or someone with a bike. The most amount of people will be able to use the entrance, regardless of if they have a disability or not.

Why is this important for web design?

You may be wondering what universal design or the social model has to do with creating websites. You may not need a screen reader or text enlargement, but considering these features from the start is important to ensure that everyone can access your content. You should adapt your content to be accessible to everyone rather than expecting people to adapt to your web content. Instead of expecting some people with low vision to zoom in on your text to be able to read it properly, you could consider using a base text size that maximizes the amount of people who can see it, like 20 pixels.

Keeping all of this in mind is both beneficial to your potential audience, but also to you. It saves you from having to go back and make changes if you just do it from the beginning. Doing everything possible to make sure that your website is accessible to the widest possible audience widens your potential audience and reaches the most amount of people possible. And not to mention that making your web content universally accessible is just the right thing to do.
