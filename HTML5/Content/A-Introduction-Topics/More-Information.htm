Types of Disabilities and Who Benefits from AD?

Audio descriptions, captions, and assistive technologies help people with a wide range of disabilities, not just permanent ones. Whether someone is blind, has a broken arm, or is watching a video without headphones in a quiet environment, accessibility benefits everyone.

Visual Disabilities

This includes people who are blind, have low vision, are colorblind, or sensitive to bright and flashing lights. Audio description would help them understand visual parts of a video that they would otherwise not be able to, and also give them warnings about strobe lights. For example, users who are both deaf and blind can also use screen readers or Braille displays to help them better comprehend the content.

Auditory Disabilities

This included people who are deaf or hard of hearing, who rely on closed captions. Captions show dialogue, identify who is speaking, and include important sounds like [applause] or [music playing]. There are also assistive technologies like personal listening devices (PLDs), FM systems, sound field systems, and noise-canceling headphones that help people hear more clearly.

Motor Disabilities

People with limited motor control, tremors, or muscle conditions may use tools like voice recognition software, switch devices instead of a mouse, and accessible media players that are easier to navigate.

Cognitive Disabilities

People with learning disabilities, attention disorders, or memory challenges benefit from the accessibility features offered by audio descriptions, captions, and ATs.

Audio description can help by explaining important visual details in a video. Hearing actions, expressions, or on-screen text described out loud provides extra context and can make the content easier to understand.

Closed captions let people simultaneously read and hear the information, which can help with focus and comprehension, especially if someone has trouble processing verbal information quickly.

Tools like audio recorders and note-taking apps can also support users by allowing them to record lectures or discussions and listen back later to reinforce and review information. Some apps even sync notes with the recording, so students can click on their notes and hear what was being said.
